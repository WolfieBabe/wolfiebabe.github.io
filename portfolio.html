Capstone Portfolio: Political, Social, and Environmental Responsibility - Protect Trans Rights

This was my final project of the Girls Who Code SIP, the activist toolkit. It combined all of the previous lessons: HTML, CSS, and JS. It serves as a rudimentary introduction to campaigning for transgender rights. The website features a basic Home page; a resources page with an embedded twitter feed to @TrackTransBills and a custom message generator as well as a small "about" page. Knowing the injustices minority groups, such as transgender people, face in today's climate, I feel that is one's duty to educate themselves and excersice their privalege and voice to help by sociopolitical means. Without doing so, these groups will be subjected to more violence and opression than they already experience.

Capstone Portfolio: Ethical and Responsible Behaviors - Spectacles of Suffering: The Hypocrisy of Martyrdom in Kafka’s A Hunger Artist

The doc you see below was a paper that I wrote for my Introduction to Literature class. In this paper I recognized and dissected the the hypocritical nature of society through Franz Kafka's lens, particularly focusing on the religious aspects therein. Furthermore, I argued that by commodifing suffering, society strips it of its spiritual, moral, and ethical value. I showed how, through the use of religious symbolism and metaphorical contrast, Kafka exposes how institutions ethically fail by exploiting suffering for control, status, or entertainment. Consequently, this speaks to a wider issue of the authenticity, integrity, and moral cost of performative martyrdom.


Capstone Portfolio: Diversity Awareness - Caricature and How It Relates to All: An Analysis of Driving Miss Daisy by Alfred Uhry

The essay below is another paper I wrote for my Introduction to Literature class. In my critique of Driving Miss Daisy I demonstrated that Alfred Uhry illustrates how institutionalized racism and classism did not simply vanish in the past but exist in a more subtle presence today. Through the analysis of internalized prejudice in Daisy and assertion of dignity in Hoke, I emphasized the necessity of introspection and self-awareness as to the nature of our own unconscious prejudice. Additionally, I demonstrated that the metamorphosis of solidarity by the marginalized groups, as reflected in Uhry, is a reminder that we need the active confrontation of discrimination, the responsible handling of privilege to evolve. Today, it invites us to construct sincere cross-community allyship, promote equity in day to day dealings, and be on full alert against the re-disguising of systematic oppression.
